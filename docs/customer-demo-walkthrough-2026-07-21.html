⚠ The one rule for a live demo

This app is deliberately built so that nothing goes out to a real owner, tenant, or vendor without a human pressing a final button. That is a selling point — but it also means two surfaces can produce a real effect. During the demo you will show them, not fire them:

  • Renewal → Live notices “Create Gmail draft” — creates a real (unsent) draft against real tenant data. In the demo, preview it and stop. Don’t click Create Gmail draft unless you are on a lease you control and want a real draft.
  • Communications → “Send exact linked reply” — the one path that actually sends email. In the demo, walk up to the confirmation card and describe it; do not tick the box and send unless the recipient is an address you own.

Everything else (Ask, Spaces, the renewal desk & reconciliation run, the Approval Queue, Maintenance test tickets, the draft composer, roles & scopes, connections) runs on sample / test / simulated data and is 100% safe to click live.

ACT 0

Pre-flight & the 60-second pitch

Do this 10 minutes before the call so the demo opens instantly and you never fumble a login in front of the customer.

0.1 Before the call — presenter checklist

  • Sign in to the app with your Admin @pmikcmetro.com Google account so you land on the Console. (A brand-new account defaults to Editor; you want Admin so every tab and gate is visible.)
  • Confirm the top nav shows all eight tabs: Console · Spaces · Lease Renewal · Maintenance · Approval Queue · Communications · Connections · Admin. If a tab is missing, you’re not on an Admin account.
  • Open Approval Queue once to confirm there are a few test items waiting (the demo is livelier with items in it). If it’s empty, you’ll still demo the flow — just narrate it.
  • Have a second browser profile or an incognito window ready if you want to show the signed-out sign-in screen without logging yourself out.
  • Close noisy tabs. Zoom the browser to ~110–125% so the room can read it.
  • Re-read the Demo-safety quick card at the bottom.
▶ Tip

This is a real, running production build — not a slide deck or a mockup. Say that out loud early; it lands.

0.2 The opening line (say this first)

Say

“What you’re about to see is your operation’s front door. Instead of ten tabs and a spreadsheet, your team asks one question box what to do, and the app routes the work to the right place — renewals, maintenance, approvals, communications — with the property data already attached. The theme you’ll notice throughout is the app does the drafting and the checking; a person always makes the decision and presses the button. Nothing leaves the building on its own.”

ACT 3

Lease Renewal — the centerpiece

This is the flagship. Show how the app turns a renewal window into a guided, step-by-step desk that catches data conflicts, routes decisions to a human, and ends by drafting the tenant email for you to send — never for itself to send.

▶ How the data map works here (accurate in prod)

The renewal desk and per-lease workspace run on sample data (they show a “Sample data” chip) so you can walk the workflow before live sources are connected. The live equivalents are already built and use real data: Live review reads real RentVine + Sheet, and Live notices (§3.5) creates a real unsent Gmail draft. Two guardrails are deliberate and stay: owner and tenant emails are draft-only (a person opens Gmail and sends), and the renewal tracking sheet stays read-only — the app records your write-back authorization for a later, owner-approved sheet write.

ACT 4

Approval Queue — the governance backbone

The single place every consequential decision funnels through — with the right person, a required reason, and a permanent record. This is the “how do I stay in control at scale” answer.

4.1 The decision inbox

Do

Click Approval Queue. Read the landing, then expand Other views.

See

A clean inbox: “N thing(s) need your decision, most urgent first.” Under Other views are three tabs — All items / Renewal reviews (N) / Write-back proposals (N). Open All items for the full list, detail panel, filters, and a bulk bar.

Say

“Everything across the app that needs a yes/no — a renewal decision, a write-back, a high-risk action — collects here, most urgent first. One place to look; nothing slips.”

Tip

The action beats (4.2–4.6) need items in the queue. If it’s empty on this build, narrate from a selected item — the buttons, reasons, and gates still demonstrate — or seed a few test items before the call.

4.2 A routine approval — one click

Do

In All items, select a plain low/medium item and click Approve.

See

One click, no reason required; status flips to Approved, an Activity row is written, the item collapses out of the active list. (“Approval changes this app decision only” — no send, no record write.)

Say

“Low-stakes work stays fast — one click. The friction scales with the risk, which you’ll see next.”

4.3 A high-risk approval — confirm and a written reason

Do

Select a High-risk item and click Approve. Try to submit with the reason blank first, then add one.

See

A browser confirm: “This is a High-risk approval. Approve this queue item?” A reason is required — blank is refused (“High-risk approval requires a reason.”), and the reason is now enforced on the server, not just the screen, so the audit trail can never hold an un-reasoned high-risk approval.

Paste as the high-risk approval reason
Confirmed the amount and recipient with the owner by phone on 2026-07-20; approving for execution.
Say

“For anything high-risk, one click isn’t enough. The app makes you confirm and write down why — and that reason is captured permanently. If a decision is ever questioned months later, there’s a name, a timestamp, and a reason attached to it.”

4.4 Deny, Return, Snooze — every path leaves a reason

Do

On a review item, try Deny, Return, and Snooze — each with the reason (and, for Snooze, the date) blank first, then filled.

See

Blank is always refused — “Deny requires a reason,” “Return for Revision requires a reason,” “Snooze requires a date and reason.” Filled → Denied (terminal), Returned (back to the submitter), or Snoozed (with the date). Every outcome is logged.

Say

“There’s no silent ‘no.’ Deny, send back, or defer — each one records why, so the person on the other end knows what to fix and you keep a clean history.”

4.5 The right person decides — role & self-approval guards

Do

Try to approve an item that’s assigned to you.

See

Refused — “You cannot approve your own assigned item.” Only the required approver or an Admin can approve; linked/consequential executions require Admin (“Admin role is required for this consequential execution.”).

Say

“You can’t rubber-stamp your own work — the app enforces separation of duties. The person who prepares a thing isn’t the person who approves it.”

4.6 Bulk actions, safely capped

Do

Tick several items and use the bulk bar; try to select more than 50.

See

Past 50 → “Bulk actions are limited to 50 visible items.” After Apply → “Bulk action finished: X updated, Y skipped, Z failed.” A bulk Execute is offered but every item is skipped with “…No external write was attempted” — the app will not batch-fire external effects.

Say

“You can move fast in bulk — but even bulk can’t trigger a mass external action. That door is closed on purpose.”

ACT 6

Communications — governed email, not a second inbox

The trust centerpiece for anyone nervous about “AI sending emails.” Show that the app drafts and assists everywhere, but the only way anything sends is one exact reply, to an already-linked thread, behind a one-time human confirmation.

6.1 Frame it: a governed lane for workflow email

Do

Click Communications. Read the header and the footer.

See

Header “Workflow Communications” with the boundary stated plainly: “Unrelated mail, generic compose, and mailbox management stay in Gmail.” A connection status line (“Connected as <mailbox>” or “Waiting on Gmail access”) and a footer noting that inbox search, recent-inbox browsing, free-form compose, and arbitrary labels live in Gmail.

Say

“This is a focused, governed lane for the specific workflow-linked messages the app helps with. Everyday inbox work stays in Gmail, and the app tells you that plainly.”

6.2 The app drafts; you send — the anticipatory composer

Do

Open the Admin tool “Anticipatory draft.” Pick a reply pattern, fill Sender / Category / Subject / Facts, and click Compose draft. Then click Copy draft. Finally, try a hard-excluded Category (Owner money / Legal-notices / Tenant disputes).

See

A draft whose first line is exactly “Draft — Review before sending,” with any missing fact shown as “Needs Verification: …” Copy draft copies it (it can’t send — no mailbox is touched). A hard-excluded category is refused before the model even runs: “Refused before the model: …”

Say

“It writes the reply for you and marks every unknown ‘needs verification’ so nothing is fabricated — but all it can do is hand you text to copy. And for the sensitive categories — owner money, legal notices, tenant disputes — it refuses to draft at all. Those always go through a person.”

6.3 Summarize and triage — all read-only

Do

Show Template & triage workspace (Evaluate), Thread summary (Summarize thread), and Simulated email chain (add a reply, then refresh — it resets).

See

Suggested labels and a draft preview; a Summary / Waiting-on / Suggested-next-action; and a browser-only chain that vanishes on refresh (“no Gmail API, mailbox, database, or external delivery is involved”). None of these can send.

Say

“It can read a long thread and tell you what it’s about, who’s waiting, and what to do next — as a suggestion. Great for clearing a backlog, and it can’t touch the real mailbox while doing it.”

6.4 The one and only send — exact, linked, and confirmed once

Safety

This is the real send path. In a customer demo, walk up to the confirmation card and describe it. Do not tick the checkbox and send unless the recipient is an address you own.

Do

On a live maintenance ticket, expand “Linked Gmail communication,” Load linked communication → Open the thread → Request source-backed reply proposal → Review exact linked reply.

See

A confirmation card “Exact linked reply: not yet sent” showing From / To / CC / BCC / Subject / Linked thread / Confirmation expires / the exact reply body — all resolved server-side from the real thread (never the AI text directly). The required checkbox reads “I reviewed the exact mailbox, recipient, subject, and reply body.” A send returns a bodyless receipt (Message/Thread ID) and “The exact linked reply was sent once”; a duplicate returns the same receipt — no double-send.

Say

“This is the only thing in the entire app that sends an email — and look how much has to be true first: it must be an exact reply to a thread that’s already linked to a real ticket, the recipient and body are rebuilt from the real thread (not from the AI), and a human has to check a box that literally says ‘I reviewed the recipient and body.’ It sends once, and if the network hiccups it won’t double-send. That’s as controlled as outbound email gets.”

6.5 Prove the negative — no autonomous or bulk send exists

Do

Look for a “compose new email,” “send all,” or bulk-send control anywhere in Communications.

See

There is none. Generic compose is refused server-side (“New-message sending is not exposed by Workflow Communications; use an approved unsent workflow draft.”), and the Connection Center lists the legacy notification sender as “Disabled.”

Say

“There is no ‘blast an email’ button to find — not hidden, not disabled, it doesn’t exist. The app cannot email your owners or tenants on its own. Every message is a person’s decision.”
